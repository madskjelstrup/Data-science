Target 8.7 - Child Labour:

“Take immediate and effective measures to eradicate forced labour, end modern slavery and human trafficking and secure the prohibition and elimination of the worst forms of child labour, including recruitment and use of child soldiers, and by 2025 end child labour in all its forms.”

A Worldwide problem

This table shows how many children in the age of 5-17 years old that are involved in hazardous work anually. For instance in 2020 there was 79 million children doing hazardous labor, and most of these children lives in the Sub-Saharan African region (22.4 million children) and the Asian and the Pacific region (22.2 million children). It is striking how much these regions make up of the total world amount in contrast to the relative smaller number of 300.000 children in western Europe that suffer from hazardous labor.

From fields to futures: ACCEL Africa Project

The Ugandan women building a brighter tomorrow

Women participating in the ACCEL Africa project in Uganda have transformed their village by combining savings, education and community care to combat child labour and promote brighter futures for their children.

LIWULUKO, UGANDA (ILO News). In the green hills of Sironko district in eastern Uganda, a group of women are quietly transforming their community. What began as an initiative to tackle child labour through training in financial literacy and agriculture has blossomed into something much more powerful, a model of grassroots resilience and hope.

The project, implemented under the Accelerating Action for the Elimination of Child Labour in Supply Chains in Africa (ACCEL Africa) project and funded by the Ministry of Foreign Affairs of the Netherlands, helped women like Namboozo Oliver to reimagine what their lives and those of their children could be.

“I didn’t know we were subjecting our children to child labour,” Oliver said, recalling the days when her 10-year-old carried heavy bananas to market to support the family. “Now I know that we need to let children be children. They need to go to school, to grow well, and to dream.”

      ACCEL Africa - Something can be done

      What makes the story of Liwuluko exceptional is its sustainability. This is not an intervention driven from the outside, but one rooted in local leadership and collective responsibility.

      As more parents witnessed the benefits, community interest and participation have grown. Building on these achievements, ACCEL Africa will continue to support community-driven initiatives like this one, strengthening women’s economic empowerment and promoting improved livelihoods for families working on the coffee and tea supply chains in Uganda.

      Yet, lasting change requires collective commitment. Governments, local leaders, the private sector and development partners all have a role to play in sustaining and scaling up these community models. By investing in women’s leadership, supporting early childhood education and care, and creating decent work for adults, we can help build a generation free from child labour. Together, we can turn the story of Liwuluko into the story of every community across Africa.
